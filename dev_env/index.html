But...does it mean that I have to contribute something towards the software effort?
No. Most Open Source Software is offered free to use and modify, as per offered by the software author via the license, without any requirements to contribute.
Most who use OSS would not even need to modify the software, and find that it would fullfill their needs adequately. Otherwise, they can often find the specific variation they would need, or find another piece of software to fit the gap.
It is usually only for advanced developers who find the need to extend software capabilities to contribute code to fulfilling missing needs.
Although once involved, many find it much easier to give contribution. For example, documentation on how to use the software is almost always welcome. So feel free to download open source software and use it as much as you can. The authors make their software openly available for just that reason!

So, the main question stands; Can OSS be leveraged by Instructional Designers and Education Technologist in implementing elearning?
eLearning is implemented by Instructional Designers, Education Technologist, and Software Developers. Often what distinguishes what kind of elearning depends on the context; respectively Training and Professional Development, Academia, and machine learning
Even professionals in this field, that are accustomed to proprietary software, can get up to speed quickly, as the entire industry continues to converge towards standards and easier implementations.
But isn't it hard to implement OSS?
Everything is relative. With a bit of knowledge, good design, and consideration, it is often easier than having to learn about and deploy monolithic software.
Plus, professionals can fall into the software trap, where their experience and skills are confined to a specific software brand. OSS embodies the work of the world, and those are some broad shoulders to stand on.
What is certain, is there is more flexibility and control with implementing OSS. If you're interested in knowing more, you may want to check out Richard Stallman's presentation with TEDx. It's the ultimate view of Open Source Software. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ag1AKIl_2GM
